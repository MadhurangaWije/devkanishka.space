Encapsulated organisms & aspleniaBacteriology · Theme 1

Mnemonic

"SHiN": Strep. pneumoniae, H. influenzae type b, Neisseria meningitidis. Same capsule biology → same two risks: OPSI after splenectomy/asplenia, and community-acquired pyogenic meningitis in adults.

Why: capsule blocks complement/phagocyte recognition → needs opsonisation (spleen's job). No spleen ⇒ unchecked bacteraemia ⇒ OPSI (~50% mortality).

Choosing the right diagnostic testBacteriology · Theme 2

High yield

Day 1 of fever → antigen or PCR (organism/antigen present, antibody not yet). Day 7–14 / convalescent → serology (IgM has had time to rise).

This single heuristic resolves dengue, leptospirosis, COVID, and most viral-serology MCQs across both lessons.

  • Smear/stain — needs high organism numbers at an accessible site (Gram, ZN, India ink)
  • Antigen — fast, but only positive while antigen load is high (early)
  • Serology — a test of the past days–weeks, not "right now"
  • PCR/NAAT — most sensitive early; detects the organism directly regardless of host response

Toxin-mediated vs immune-mediated diseaseBacteriology · Theme 3

Mnemonic

Disease named for the toxin's effect (scalded skin, flaccid paralysis, haemolytic-uraemic) → toxin-mediated, fast onset (hours), can be reproduced by toxin alone.

Disease named for the organ it inflames weeks later (rheumatic fever, glomerulonephritis, Guillain–Barré) → immune-mediated, delayed 1–3 weeks (molecular mimicry / immune complexes), infection itself often already resolved.

Rickettsioses, Q fever & LymeBacteriology · Theme 11

COVID-19: pathophysiology & diagnosticsVirology · Theme 1

Mechanism

ACE2 entry → diffuse alveolar damage + hyaline membranes (final common ARDS pathway) + microthrombi (endothelial infection, pro-thrombotic state). Severe disease → lymphopenia, not lymphocytosis.

High yield

High CT value = low viral load. RT-PCR ≠ variant identification (needs sequencing). Antigen positivity correlates with infectivity. Antibody tests are useless in the first 3 days (same day-1-vs-day-7 timing heuristic as Theme 2 of Bacteriology).

HIV/AIDS: seroconversion, vaccines & AIDS-defining illnessVirology · Theme 2

Acute seroconversion (mononucleosis-like): fever, rash, generalised lymphadenopathy, arthralgia — driven by initial viraemia. Late-stage features (oral hairy leukoplakia, opportunistic infections) sit at the opposite end of the timeline — the classic early-vs-late contrast.

TORCH & transfusion-transmitted infectionsVirology · Theme 8

Mnemonic

TORCH: Toxoplasma, Other (syphilis, VZV, parvovirus B19), Rubella, CMV, HSV — transplacental/congenital group.

Contrast: gonorrhoea and HPV infect the neonate intrapartum (birth passage), not transplacentally — a deliberate distractor pairing.

Dengue: phases & vaccinesVirology · Theme 4

High yield — impending shock

  • Narrowing pulse pressure (≤20 mmHg) — earlier/more sensitive than falling systolic alone
  • Rising haematocrit (haemoconcentration) — monitor ≥12-hourly
  • Hypoalbuminaemia + abdominal pain
  • Imaging: gallbladder wall oedema, pleural effusion

ADE: secondary infection can be more severe (antibody-dependent enhancement). Expanded dengue syndrome = atypical severe presentations (incl. encephalopathy).

VaccineKey safety note
Dengvaxia (CYD-TDV)Seronegative recipients: silent primary infection → ADE risk on natural infection
Qdenga (TAK-003)Lower ADE risk; weaker vs DENV-3/4 in seronegative children

Zika contrast: frequently asymptomatic; risks are fetal microcephaly + Guillain-Barré, not haemorrhagic shock.

Rabies pathogenesisVirology · Theme 6

Mechanism

Rhabdovirus (not Togavirus). Local muscle replication → retrograde axonal spread to CNS (no haematogenous spread) → strongly neurotropic → travels back out to salivary glands (saliva only infectious late in incubation).

Furious rabies: hyperactivity, hydrophobia/aerophobia (pathognomonic). Paralytic ("dumb") rabies: progressive paralysis. Once symptomatic: essentially always fatal.

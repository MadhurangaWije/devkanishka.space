Theme 1

COVID-19: pathophysiology & diagnostics

Four questions (Q1, Q3, Q4, Q9) plus two predicted questions test the same two things repeatedly: the histopathology of severe COVID-19 at autopsy, and how to correctly interpret PCR, antigen and antibody results for SARS-CoV-2 — a diagnostic-test-timing question in the same family as Theme 2 of the Bacteriology lesson.

Mechanism SARS-CoV-2 binds ACE2 receptors (densely expressed on type II pneumocytes and vascular endothelium) to enter cells. Severe disease produces diffuse alveolar damage (DAD) — the same final common histological pathway seen in ARDS from any cause — with hyaline membranes lining damaged alveoli (protein-rich exudate from the injured air-blood barrier). Endothelial infection and a pro-thrombotic state (complement activation, endothelial injury, immobility) together drive widespread microthrombi in the pulmonary microvasculature, contributing to hypoxia beyond what alveolar damage alone would explain. Severity in older/elderly patients relates partly to a more dysregulated, delayed adaptive T-cell response allowing more viral replication and tissue damage before the immune system catches up — and correspondingly, severe disease shows lymphopenia, not lymphocytosis, as circulating lymphocytes are consumed/redistributed into inflamed tissue.
Q1
Regarding pathophysiology of COVID-19:
  1. SARS-CoV-2 binds to ACE2 and enters host cells
  2. Forms a hyaline membrane and diffuse alveolar exudate
  3. Microthrombi in the lungs and other tissues
  4. Lymphocytosis indicates severe infection
  5. Causes severe infection in elderly due to dysregulated T cells
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c, e — all directly from the mechanism above.

d. Lymphocytosis
Severe COVID-19 is marked by lymphopenia, one of the most consistently reported laboratory findings correlating with severity — the reverse of this option.
Q4
50-year-old COVID-19 patient with refractory hypoxia died after 7 days. Possible lung histology at autopsy:
  1. Thrombi in microvasculature
  2. Hyaline membranes in alveoli
  3. Intranuclear Cowdry bodies in type 2 pneumocytes
  4. Bronchiectasis
  5. Caseation
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c. Microthrombi and hyaline membranes as above. Intranuclear Cowdry type A inclusions in hyperplastic type II pneumocytes have been described in severe COVID-19 — worth knowing this is a shared histological feature with CMV pneumonia (also produces Cowdry-A-like inclusions in alveolar pneumocytes), so "Cowdry bodies in the lung" isn't automatically CMV on an exam question — check the clinical context.

d. Bronchiectasis
A chronic structural airway disease that develops over months to years — not a feature of a 7-day acute illness.
e. Caseation
The histological hallmark of TB granulomas, not viral pneumonia — a cross-disease distractor testing whether you keep acute viral ARDS and chronic granulomatous disease separate.
Q9
True regarding COVID-19:
  1. Disease is more severe in children than in adults
  2. Causes ARDS
  3. Enters the cell via the ACE2 receptor
  4. Is a beta coronavirus
  5. Can cause pulmonary thrombosis
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, d, e. SARS-CoV-2 is a beta-coronavirus (same genus as SARS-CoV-1 and MERS-CoV), using ACE2 entry, capable of progressing to ARDS with pulmonary thrombosis.

a. Worse in children
The reverse — severe disease is overwhelmingly more common in adults, particularly the elderly and those with comorbidities; children are generally less severely affected (with the notable exception of the rare MIS-C syndrome, a separate topic).
High yield — the testing-timeline heuristic, applied to COVID RT-PCR: detects viral RNA directly; the Ct (cycle threshold) value is inversely related to viral load — a high Ct means low viral load (and generally lower infectivity), while routine diagnostic PCR does not identify the specific variant (that needs sequencing). Antigen tests: fast, but need a high viral load to turn positive, so positivity broadly correlates with higher infectivity — good for "is this person infectious right now," weaker for early/low-viral-load infection. Antibody (serology) tests: reflect past infection/immune response, not useful for diagnosing acute infection in the first few days.
Q3
Regarding COVID-19:
  1. Virus envelope is resistant against disinfectants
  2. PCR cannot specify the variant
  3. High CT value indicates low infectivity
  4. Antigen positivity correlates with infectivity
  5. Anti-SARS-CoV-2 antibody is used in acute infection
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, d — directly from the testing-timeline box above.

a. Envelope resistant to disinfectants
SARS-CoV-2 is an enveloped virus — enveloped viruses are generally more susceptible, not resistant, to disinfectants and detergents, which disrupt the lipid envelope.
e. Antibody used in acute infection
Serology reflects the developing immune response and is a test of the past, not the present — not useful for diagnosing acute infection in the first few days, when RT-PCR/antigen tests are the right choice.
Theme 2

HIV/AIDS: seroconversion, monitoring & AIDS-defining illness

The single biggest cluster in the virology deck — eight questions (Q2, Q5, Q7, Q18, Q23, Q24, Q26, Q39) plus two reference tables and two predicted questions. Three distinct sub-skills are being tested: recognising acute seroconversion as a syndrome, knowing which tests monitor established disease vs which ones diagnose it, and correctly sorting conditions into AIDS-defining vs merely "suggests immunosuppression."

Mechanism Acute HIV (seroconversion) syndrome occurs roughly 2–4 weeks (some sources say up to 6–8 weeks) after exposure, when the initial burst of viral replication triggers a non-specific flu-like/mononucleosis-like illness: fever, rash (maculopapular or vesiculopustular), pharyngitis, generalised lymphadenopathy, and polyarthralgia. It is transient and self-resolving, often going completely unrecognised. This is fundamentally different from the late-stage features that appear only after years of untreated infection and progressive CD4 decline — oral hairy leukoplakia, persistent generalised lymphadenopathy (PGL, classically non-tender, unlike the tender/reactive nodes of many other infections), and eventually the AIDS-defining opportunistic diseases once CD4 falls below the thresholds where specific pathogens escape control.
Q2
Features characteristic of HIV seroconversion:
  1. Reduced CD4 counts in peripheral blood
  2. Oral hairy leukoplakia
  3. Vesiculopustular or maculopapular rash
  4. Multiple small-joint polyarthritis
  5. Generalized lymphadenopathy
Show reasoning

Correct: c, d, e — the acute seroconversion triad above.

a. Reduced CD4 in peripheral blood
Acute seroconversion actually shows a transient CD4 dip with rebound alongside a characteristic CD4:CD8 ratio inversion, rather than the progressive, sustained CD4 depletion of chronic untreated disease — the option as phrased describes the chronic-disease pattern, not the acute one.
b. Oral hairy leukoplakia
A late-stage EBV-driven feature reflecting more advanced immunosuppression — not part of the acute seroconversion picture.
Q23 · Oct 2017
Regarding HIV seroconversion:
  1. Occurs after 6–8 weeks
  2. Persistent lymphadenopathy seen
  3. Mostly occurs without symptoms
  4. Typical maculopapular rash occurs
Show reasoning

Correct: a, c, d. Many cases of acute seroconversion are mild or entirely unrecognised ("mostly without symptoms" — or symptoms too non-specific to be attributed to HIV), with rash as a recognised feature when symptomatic.

b. Persistent lymphadenopathy
Seroconversion causes acute, generalised lymphadenopathy that typically settles — the persistent generalised lymphadenopathy (PGL) label is reserved for the later chronic phase, a distinct clinical entity from acute seroconversion nodes.
Q5
Direct actions by HIV on host cells:
  1. Neutropenia
  2. Hypochlorhydria
  3. Cardiomyopathy
  4. Eosinophilic interstitial pneumonitis
  5. Painful sensory neuropathy
Show reasoning

Correct: a only. HIV can directly infect bone marrow stromal/progenitor elements and cause cytopenias, including neutropenia, as a direct cytopathic effect.

b–e. The rest
Hypochlorhydria, cardiomyopathy, interstitial pneumonitis and painful sensory neuropathy are all recognised HIV-associated complications, but they arise indirectly — via opportunistic infection, drug toxicity (notably some older antiretrovirals causing peripheral neuropathy), or immune dysregulation — rather than being a direct cytopathic effect of the virus itself on that tissue. The question is specifically testing "direct" vs "indirect" causation, a distinction worth carrying into any HIV complication question.

Monitoring established HIV vs diagnosing it

A distinct skill from recognising seroconversion: once someone is a known HIV patient, which tests track their disease and response to treatment?

Q24 · Oct 2017
Tests used for HIV infection monitoring:
  1. p24 antigen
  2. HIV antibodies
  3. RNA viral load
  4. CD8 cell count
  5. CD4 cell count
Show reasoning

Correct: c, e. Longitudinal disease monitoring and ART response are tracked by plasma HIV RNA viral load (falling toward undetectable = treatment working) and CD4 count (immune status, opportunistic-infection risk, and prophylaxis thresholds).

a, b. p24 antigen, HIV antibodies
These are diagnostic tools (used to establish the initial diagnosis, especially in the window period where p24 antigen can be positive before antibodies develop) rather than tools for ongoing monitoring of a known patient.
d. CD8 count
Not used as a routine monitoring parameter in standard HIV care algorithms.
Q39 · April 2014
HIV/AIDS:
  1. Cryptococcus is the commonest cause for meningitis
  2. Occurrence of oral thrush in an otherwise healthy patient indicates progression of disease
  3. Pentamidine is the drug of choice for PCP
  4. In persistent generalized lymphadenopathy, lymph nodes are tender
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b. In advanced HIV, Cryptococcus neoformans is a leading cause of meningitis (linking back to the Bacteriology lesson's CSF-pattern theme). New-onset oral candidiasis in a previously well patient is clinically significant — it signals meaningful new immunosuppression and should prompt HIV testing/staging review.

c. Pentamidine as first-line PCP therapy
Co-trimoxazole (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) is first-line for Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia; pentamidine is reserved as an alternative for those who cannot tolerate co-trimoxazole.
d. PGL nodes are tender
PGL nodes are classically non-tender — tenderness would point more toward an acute reactive/infective process rather than the chronic immune-activation lymphadenopathy of PGL.

ART thresholds, live vaccines & AIDS-defining illness

Q18
Regarding HIV:
  1. Cervical cancer is an AIDS-defining illness
  2. HIV-induced nephropathy is an indication for ART irrespective of the CD4 count
  3. ART should only be started once CD4 count is below 300
  4. Live vaccines are absolutely contraindicated in AIDS
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b. Invasive cervical cancer is on the AIDS-defining list; HIV- associated nephropathy is treated with ART regardless of CD4 count because untreated it progresses to renal failure independent of the usual CD4-driven staging logic.

c. ART only below CD4 <300
Outdated — current guidelines recommend ART for everyone diagnosed with HIV, regardless of CD4 count ("treat all" strategy), not gated to a CD4 threshold.
d. Live vaccines "absolutely" contraindicated
Not absolute — some live vaccines can be given to HIV patients who are clinically stable and have adequately reconstituted CD4 counts above specific thresholds (see the table below); it's conditional, not blanket, contraindication.

Q7
AIDS defining illnesses:
  1. Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PJP)
  2. PMLE (or PML?)
  3. CMV retinitis
  4. Cerebral toxoplasmosis
  5. Chronic necrotizing aspergillosis
Show reasoning

Correct: a, c, d. PJP, CMV retinitis, and cerebral toxoplasmosis all appear on the canonical CDC/WHO AIDS-defining conditions list.

b. PMLE / PML
If the intended condition is PML (progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy, caused by JC virus), that is AIDS-defining — but as written ("PMLE," polymorphic light eruption, a photosensitive skin condition unrelated to HIV staging) it is not, which is exactly the ambiguity the original question flags. Know both possibilities and don't confuse the abbreviations.
e. Chronic necrotising aspergillosis
Not on the standard AIDS-defining list, despite occurring in immunocompromised hosts generally.
Q26 · April 2017
AIDS defining conditions:
  1. Oral candidiasis
  2. Herpes zoster
  3. Pulmonary TB
  4. Cerebral lymphoma
  5. Severe neutropenia
Show reasoning

Correct: c, d. Pulmonary TB (any site of TB, in fact) and primary CNS lymphoma are both AIDS-defining.

a. Oral candidiasis, b. Localised herpes zoster
Both indicate meaningful immunosuppression and should prompt HIV testing/staging (as in Q39 above) but are not themselves AIDS-defining — the AIDS-defining list requires oesophageal/pulmonary candidiasis (not simple oral thrush) and disseminated/ multidermatomal zoster, not a single localised dermatome.

Theme 3

The herpesvirus family (VZV, HSV, CMV, EBV)

Five questions (Q8, Q10, Q11, Q12, Q21) test the herpesvirus family — all DNA viruses, all capable of latency and reactivation, but each with its own signature disease association and its own serology-interpretation rules.

Mechanism All human herpesviruses are DNA viruses that establish lifelong latency after primary infection (in sensory ganglia for HSV/VZV, in B cells for EBV, in myeloid precursors for CMV), then can reactivate under immunosuppression. The serology principle that recurs across this whole group: IgM (or a rising/low-avidity IgG) = recent/acute infection; high-avidity IgG = past infection — avidity testing exists precisely because IgM alone can persist or reappear during reactivation, so avidity helps distinguish a true new infection (important in pregnancy) from an old one.
Q8
Chickenpox (varicella) transmission:
  1. Direct contact with ruptured vesicles
  2. Direct contact with crusted lesions
  3. Fomites
  4. Transplacental route
  5. Droplet inhalation
Show reasoning

Correct: a, d, e. Varicella spreads mainly by respiratory droplets and direct contact with vesicular fluid (which is teeming with infectious virus); congenital infection occurs via transplacental spread when a non-immune mother is infected during pregnancy.

b. Crusted lesions
Once vesicles have crusted over, they are no longer considered infectious — infectivity is tied to the fluid-filled vesicular stage, not the healing/crusted stage.
c. Fomites
VZV is relatively fragile outside the host and not considered a significant fomite-transmitted virus in standard teaching, unlike some more environmentally stable pathogens.
Q21 · April 2018
Regarding chickenpox:
  1. Incubation period is less than a week
  2. Viral DNA can be isolated from vesicular secretions
  3. Varicella does not cause pneumonia in immunocompetent patients
  4. When a mother acquires infection at term she should be given immunoglobulin
Show reasoning

Correct: b, d. Vesicle-fluid PCR is a standard diagnostic method. Maternal varicella around the time of delivery risks severe neonatal varicella (the neonate is exposed without the benefit of maternal antibody transfer time), so VZIG is given to the mother/neonate in that specific peripartum window.

a. Incubation <1 week
Varicella's incubation period is considerably longer, typically about 10–21 days.
c. No pneumonia in immunocompetent patients
Varicella pneumonia is a well-recognised, sometimes severe complication even in immunocompetent adults (more so than in healthy children) — it is not restricted to the immunocompromised.
Q11
Regarding cytomegalovirus (CMV):
  1. Does not produce symptomatic clinical picture
  2. Owl's eye intranuclear bodies in histology
  3. Microcephaly in fetus
  4. Congenital infection causes periventricular calcification
  5. IgG shows acute infection
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, d. CMV is the classic cause of the "owl's eye" intranuclear inclusion on histology, and congenital CMV can produce microcephaly and periventricular calcification — one of the most exam-tested TORCH imaging findings.

a. "Does not produce symptomatic disease"
CMV infection is often asymptomatic in immunocompetent hosts, but it absolutely can cause significant disease (mononucleosis-like illness, congenital infection, retinitis/colitis in immunosuppression) — "does not" overstates it into an absolute that isn't true.
e. IgG shows acute infection
IgG alone (especially high-avidity IgG) indicates past infection — acute infection needs IgM or demonstrated low-avidity IgG or seroconversion (negative-to-positive) to be confirmed.
Q10
EBV causes:
  1. B cell lymphoma
  2. Nasopharyngeal carcinoma
  3. Infectious mononucleosis
  4. Plasmacytoma
  5. Kaposi's sarcoma
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c. EBV is linked to Burkitt lymphoma and other B-cell lymphomas, nasopharyngeal carcinoma, and of course classic infectious mononucleosis.

d. Plasmacytoma, e. Kaposi's sarcoma
Neither is EBV-driven — Kaposi's sarcoma is caused by HHV-8, a different herpesvirus entirely, and plasmacytoma is not established as virally driven in the way EBV-associated lymphomas are. A useful contrast to memorise alongside EBV: EBV → Burkitt/Hodgkin/nasopharyngeal; HHV-8 → Kaposi's sarcoma/primary effusion lymphoma.
Q12
Herpes virus infection:
  1. Acyclovir resistant herpes virus seen
  2. HSV-2 primarily causes genital herpes
  3. High avidity IgG antibodies to CMV indicate recent infection
  4. IgM to viral capsid antigen of EBV is diagnostic of acute infection
  5. Herpesviruses are DNA viruses
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, d, e. Acyclovir-resistant HSV is well described, particularly in immunocompromised hosts (usually via thymidine kinase mutations). HSV-2 remains the predominant cause of genital herpes (though HSV-1 genital infection is increasingly recognised). EBV VCA (viral capsid antigen) IgM is the standard acute-infection marker. All herpesviruses are DNA viruses.

c. High-avidity CMV IgG = recent infection
Inverted — high avidity indicates a mature, past antibody response; low-avidity IgG is what points toward recent/acute infection, since avidity maturation takes months to develop.
Q13
Regarding viruses:
  1. Congenital Parvovirus B19 infection causes congenital birth defects
  2. Hepatitis viruses can be killed by autoclaving
  3. High avidity IgG is seen in acute CMV infection
  4. In acute hepatitis anti-HBs antibody is present
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b. Parvovirus B19 can cause fetal anaemia, non-immune hydrops fetalis, and structural malformations following maternal infection in pregnancy. Autoclaving (pressurised steam sterilisation) reliably inactivates hepatitis viruses along with essentially all other pathogens — a basic sterilisation fact worth not overthinking.

c. High-avidity IgG in acute CMV
Same inverted-avidity trap as Q12 — repeated because it's clearly a favourite.
d. Anti-HBs in acute hepatitis B
Anti-HBs is the recovery/immunity marker — it appears after clearance of HBsAg (or after vaccination), not during acute active infection, when HBsAg itself (plus IgM anti-HBc) is what you'd expect to find positive.
Aside — transmission routes across common childhood viruses Worth pinning next to the herpesvirus family since it tests the same "know your transmission route" instinct: respiratory droplet spread — mumps, measles, rubella, varicella (Theme 3, above); faeco-oral — poliovirus; vector-borne — yellow fever (mosquito), unlike the others in that same historical MCQ list.
Q14
Viruses spread via the respiratory route:
  1. Polio
  2. Mumps
  3. Measles
  4. Yellow fever
  5. Rubella
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, e. Mumps, measles and rubella spread predominantly by respiratory droplets. Poliovirus is primarily faeco-oral; yellow fever is transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes — both are transmission-route outliers relative to the "classic childhood respiratory exanthem" group.

Theme 4

Arboviruses: dengue, Zika & Japanese encephalitis

Seven questions (Q16, Q17, Q25, Q27, Q28, Q30, Q35) plus a vaccine reference table and a predicted question cover the mosquito-borne viruses. Dengue dominates numerically — appropriate for a Sri Lankan exam — and its questions cluster around one core clinical skill: recognising the critical (leaking) phase before it progresses to shock.

Mechanism Dengue has three clinical phases: febrile (days 1–3, ~2–7 days total), critical/ leaking (typically around defervescence, days 3–7 — plasma leaks out of the vasculature as capillary permeability increases), and recovery (reabsorption of leaked fluid). The critical phase is where patients deteriorate, and where careful fluid management matters most. Antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE) explains why a second dengue infection with a different serotype can be more severe than the first: non-neutralising antibody from the first infection binds the new serotype without neutralising it, and instead helps the virus enter Fc-receptor- bearing cells more efficiently, amplifying viral load and the resulting inflammatory response.
High yield — impending shock Warning signs that plasma leakage is progressing toward dengue shock: narrowing pulse pressure (≤20 mmHg — a earlier and more sensitive sign than a falling systolic pressure alone), rising haematocrit (haemoconcentration from plasma loss — monitor at least 12-hourly in at-risk patients), hypoalbuminaemia with abdominal pain, and imaging signs of leakage such as gallbladder wall oedema or pleural effusion. Expanded dengue syndrome covers atypical severe presentations, including encephalopathy, that fall outside the classic haemorrhagic/shock picture.
Q16
Regarding dengue fever:
  1. Bradycardia and low blood pressure in 3rd week indicates myocarditis
  2. Vaccine is recommended in children less than 12 years in Sri Lanka
  3. Rising WBC count indicates entry into the critical period
  4. Incubation period is 2–3 weeks
  5. Second infection may be severe due to preformed antibodies
Show reasoning

Correct: e only — the ADE mechanism above.

a. Week 3 bradycardia/hypotension
Dengue's acute illness (febrile → critical → recovery) typically resolves within about 1–2 weeks; "third week" sits outside the usual acute-phase timeline the question bank is testing.
b. Vaccine in children <12 in Sri Lanka
Current dengue vaccines are generally restricted to those with evidence of prior dengue infection (to avoid ADE risk in seronegative recipients — see the vaccine table below) and specific age ranges, not a blanket recommendation for all children under 12.
c. Rising WBC → critical period
Inverted — the critical phase is typically marked by a falling white cell count (and falling platelets), not a rise.
d. Incubation 2–3 weeks
Dengue's incubation period is much shorter, typically 4–7 days.
Q17
Regarding dengue:
  1. Mean duration of febrile phase is 7 days
  2. Plasma leakage should be suspected when a patient has abdominal pain with low plasma albumin
  3. Haematocrit should be monitored 12-hourly
  4. Rise in haematocrit >25% is a definite indication for colloids
  5. When there is encephalopathy, expanded dengue syndrome should be suspected
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, e — the impending-shock and expanded-syndrome points above.

a. Febrile phase mean 7 days
Typically shorter — around 2–7 days, often quoted as 3–5 days on average.
d. Hct rise >25% = "definite" indication for colloids
Fluid choice (crystalloid vs colloid) depends on the overall clinical picture (shock status, trend, response to initial fluids), not a single haematocrit cut-off applied rigidly — "definite indication" overstates a single number's role in a nuanced bedside decision.
Q35 · Oct 2015
Dengue leaking phase — features suggestive of impending shock:
  1. Narrow pulse pressure ≤ 20 mmHg
  2. Platelets less than 140,000/mm³
  3. Gallbladder wall oedema
  4. Left-sided pleural effusion
  5. Increasing haematocrit more than 10%
Show reasoning

Correct: a, c, e — the impending-shock triad again, repeated because it's the single highest-yield dengue fact tested in this deck.

b. Platelets <140,000
Thrombocytopenia is common in dengue generally but is not specific enough on its own to signal impending shock — many patients have low platelets throughout the illness without progressing to shock.
d. Left-sided pleural effusion
Dengue plasma leakage classically produces a right-sided (or bilateral) pleural effusion more consistently than an isolated left-sided one.
Q30 · April 2017
Diagnosis of dengue:
  1. Presence of both IgM and IgG indicates secondary dengue
  2. Using two samples in immunochromatography increases the sensitivity
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b. Concurrent IgM with a high-titre IgG suggests a secondary infection (the pre-existing IgG from the first infection responds fast on re-exposure); paired or repeated rapid tests over the illness course improve overall sensitivity compared with a single sample at one time point — the same "test at the right time" principle from Theme 2 of the Bacteriology lesson.

Zika & Japanese encephalitis — the other two arboviruses tested

Q27 · April 2017
Zika:
  1. Haemorrhagic manifestations
  2. Guillain-Barré syndrome
  3. Cross-reacts with dengue NS1 antigen
  4. Causes microcephaly in fetus when infected in pregnancy
  5. Most infections are asymptomatic
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, d, e. Zika is usually mild/asymptomatic (unlike dengue's more florid febrile illness), but carries two signature severe associations: Guillain–Barré syndrome in adults, and congenital microcephaly when infection occurs during pregnancy. Serological cross-reactivity with dengue NS1 antigen (both being flaviviruses) is an important diagnostic pitfall in dengue-endemic regions.

a. Haemorrhagic manifestations
Not typical of Zika — this is more a dengue feature; Zika's illness is usually milder (low-grade fever, conjunctivitis, arthralgia, rash).
Q33 · April 2016
Zika virus:
  1. Causes ADEM
  2. Transmitted by ticks
  3. Not reported in Asian countries
  4. Causes GBS
  5. Usually fever lasts for more than 2 weeks
Show reasoning

Correct: a, d. Both ADEM (acute disseminated encephalomyelitis) and GBS are recognised autoimmune neurological complications of Zika.

b. Ticks
Zika is transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes (the same genus as dengue), not ticks.
c. Not in Asia
Zika has been reported across Asia — this option significantly understates its known geographic spread.
e. Fever >2 weeks
Zika typically causes a short, mild febrile illness of a few days, not a prolonged fever.
Q25 · Oct 2017
Japanese encephalitis:
  1. Transmitted by Culex mosquito
  2. In majority causes severe disability
  3. Treatment is acyclovir
  4. Can be diagnosed by CSF examination
Show reasoning

Correct: a, d. JE virus is transmitted by Culex mosquitoes (distinct from dengue/Zika's Aedes vector), and diagnosis often relies on detecting virus-specific IgM in CSF.

b. Majority get severe disability
The great majority of JE infections are actually asymptomatic or mild — severe encephalitis with lasting disability occurs only in a minority of infections.
c. Acyclovir
No specific antiviral treatment exists for JE (unlike HSV encephalitis, where acyclovir is genuinely indicated) — management is supportive.
Q28 · April 2017
Japanese encephalitis:
  1. Transmitted by Aedes aegypti
  2. Prevented by a live vaccine
  3. Causes a small amount of RBCs in CSF
  4. Most infections are asymptomatic
Show reasoning

Correct: b, d. A live attenuated (and inactivated) JE vaccine exists and is used in endemic programmes; most infections are subclinical, as above.

a. Aedes aegypti
The recurring vector-swap trap — JE is Culex-borne, not Aedes-borne.
c. RBCs in CSF
JE typically produces a lymphocytic pleocytosis pattern (viral encephalitis CSF profile), not a haemorrhagic CSF picture.
Theme 5

Influenza

Three questions (Q15, Q29, Q36) test influenza treatment nuances, risk groups, and the classic antigenic-drift-vs-shift distinction that explains the difference between annual epidemics and rare pandemics.

Mechanism Antigenic drift — small, gradual point mutations in haemagglutinin/neuraminidase — drives the yearly epidemics that make last year's vaccine progressively less well-matched. Antigenic shift — an abrupt, major reassortment of gene segments (possible because influenza has a segmented RNA genome, allowing two different strains co-infecting one cell, e.g. in pigs, to swap whole segments) — produces a genuinely novel haemagglutinin/neuraminidase combination the population has no prior immunity to, which is what causes pandemics.
Q36 · April 2015
Influenza:
  1. Antigenic drift results in pandemic
  2. Influenza A & B cannot be clinically differentiated
  3. Oseltamivir reduces the duration of symptoms
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c. Influenza A and B produce clinically indistinguishable illness (differentiation needs laboratory testing); oseltamivir, a neuraminidase inhibitor, modestly shortens symptom duration when started early.

a. Drift → pandemic
Pandemics arise from antigenic shift, not drift — drift is responsible for the more modest yearly epidemic pattern instead.
Q15
Regarding influenza:
  1. Oseltamivir is indicated for all pregnant women with suspected/confirmed influenza
  2. Streptococcus pneumoniae is the commonest cause for secondary bacterial pneumonia
  3. Infection can cause Parkinson's-like features
  4. Oseltamivir is contraindicated if age more than 80 years
  5. Vaccinated individuals do not need treatment
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c. Pregnancy is a recognised high-risk state for severe influenza, so antivirals are recommended regardless of trimester when infection is suspected/confirmed. S. pneumoniae remains the leading cause of secondary bacterial pneumonia post-influenza. Historically, post-encephalitic parkinsonism has been described following certain influenza outbreaks (a classic, if now less commonly cited, association).

d. Contraindicated >80 years
No such age-based contraindication exists — if anything, the elderly are a group where treatment is particularly encouraged given higher complication risk.
e. Vaccinated = no treatment needed
Vaccination reduces risk but doesn't guarantee prevention, and doesn't remove the indication for antiviral treatment if someone who was vaccinated still develops confirmed/suspected influenza, especially if at high risk of complications.
Q29 · April 2017
Influenza:
  1. Amantadine is used for treatment
  2. Adversely affects obese patients
  3. Incubation period is 1–2 days
  4. Diagnosed by PCR from throat wash samples
  5. Virus can be isolated after 7 days of infection
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, d. Obesity is an independent risk factor for severe influenza; incubation is short (1–2 days, among the shortest of common respiratory viruses); PCR on respiratory samples (throat wash/swab, nasopharyngeal) is the standard modern diagnostic approach.

a. Amantadine
Largely obsolete — widespread resistance in circulating influenza A strains means it is no longer a recommended treatment; neuraminidase inhibitors (oseltamivir) are current standard.
e. Isolation after 7 days
Viral shedding is typically highest early and diminishes over the course of the illness — while shedding can occasionally persist, particularly in immunocompromised patients, "can be isolated after 7 days" isn't the reliable teaching point being tested here.
Theme 6

Rabies

Two questions (Q22, Q32) test rabies pathogenesis in detail — worth knowing well since the disease's near-100% case fatality once symptomatic makes understanding its pre-symptomatic biology the whole point of post-exposure prophylaxis.

Mechanism Rabies virus (a Rhabdovirus, not a Togavirus) first replicates locally in muscle tissue near the bite wound, then travels retrograde along peripheral nerve axons to reach the CNS — it does not spread haematogenously. Once in the CNS it's strongly neurotropic, and only after CNS infection does it travel back out via nerves to the salivary glands, which is why saliva only becomes infectious relatively late in the incubation period, and why the virus can be detected in saliva or neural tissue as a diagnostic approach. Clinically it presents as either furious rabies (hyperactivity, aerophobia/hydrophobia — pathognomonic when present) or paralytic ("dumb") rabies (progressive paralysis, sometimes preceded by paraesthesia/ tingling at the wound site). Once clinical rabies develops, it is essentially always fatal.
Q22 · April 2018
Regarding rabies:
  1. Belongs to Togaviridae family
  2. Virus can be isolated from the saliva of infected humans
  3. Spreads to CNS via blood
  4. Is neurotropic
Show reasoning

Correct: b, d — directly from the mechanism above.

a. Togaviridae
Rabies is a Rhabdovirus (family Rhabdoviridae) — Togaviridae includes alphaviruses/rubella instead, an unrelated family.
c. Spreads via blood
Spread to the CNS is via peripheral nerve axonal transport, not the bloodstream — this distinction is the single most tested rabies pathogenesis fact.
Q32 · Oct 2016
Rabies:
  1. It multiplies within the muscle adjacent to the wound
  2. Aerophobia is pathognomonic for furious rabies
  3. Tingling sensation in dumb rabies
  4. It cannot reach saliva without multiplying in brain
  5. Clinical rabies is necessarily fatal
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, e. Local muscle replication at the wound site is the first stage; aerophobia is essentially pathognomonic when seen in furious rabies; once clinical (symptomatic) rabies develops, it is regarded as universally fatal (only a handful of survivors have ever been documented, with intensive experimental protocols).

c. "Tingling in dumb rabies" as wrong
Paraesthesia/tingling at the wound site is actually a recognised early feature more classically associated with the paralytic ("dumb") presentation in some texts — but as the deck's own answer key marks this option false, treat it as the exam-safe answer: don't over-anchor on wound-site paraesthesia as diagnostic of one specific rabies subtype, since descriptions vary between sources.
d. Cannot reach saliva without brain replication
This is actually consistent with the pathogenesis (saliva infectivity does follow CNS involvement in the classic model) — but is marked as not a correct-answer choice in the original key, most plausibly because of how absolutely the option is phrased ("cannot... without") relative to real biological variability; treat the core teaching point (CNS infection precedes salivary shedding) as solid, while noting the option's exact wording wasn't credited.
Theme 7

Molecular diagnostics: PCR fundamentals

Three questions (Q20, Q31, Q37) test the basic PCR mechanism and its clinical applications — a foundational lab-medicine topic that keeps resurfacing across virology because so many modern viral diagnoses now rest on it.

Mechanism PCR cycles through three steps exploiting a thermostable DNA polymerase (originally isolated from thermophilic bacteria, able to survive repeated heating): denaturation (heating separates the double-stranded DNA), annealing (cooling allows short, sequence- specific DNA primers to bind via complementary base pairing to the target sequence), and extension (the polymerase synthesises new complementary strands from the primers). Repeating this cycle ~20–40 times amplifies the target exponentially, which is what makes PCR sensitive enough to detect vanishingly small amounts of starting material — including in prenatal diagnosis, where only tiny quantities of fetal DNA may be available.
Q20
Steps in polymerase chain reaction (PCR):
  1. Annealing by cooling
  2. Denaturation by heating
  3. Supercoiling of DNA
  4. Transcription of genes
  5. Complementary base pairing
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, e — the core PCR cycle described above.

c. Supercoiling, d. Transcription
Neither is a step in PCR — supercoiling is a structural feature of some DNA in vivo (e.g. bacterial plasmids) unrelated to the PCR cycle, and transcription (DNA → RNA) is a distinct biological process PCR doesn't perform; PCR only amplifies DNA.
Q37 · Oct 2014
PCR:
  1. Thermostable properties of DNA polymerase are used
  2. A DNA primer is used
  3. Cannot be used for prenatal diagnosis as the amount of DNA is low in early pregnancy
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b — the core mechanism again.

c. Cannot be used prenatally due to low DNA
The opposite is true — PCR's exponential amplification is precisely what makes it well-suited to situations with very little starting DNA, including prenatal diagnostics; "low amount of DNA" is an argument for PCR, not against it.
Q31 · April 2017
Regarding PCR in diagnosis of disease:
  1. RT-PCR is used to diagnose HIV
  2. Can be used to quantify the viral load
Show reasoning

Correct: b only. PCR-based quantitative assays are the standard method for measuring HIV RNA viral load once someone is diagnosed (linking back to Theme 2's monitoring discussion).

a. RT-PCR for HIV diagnosis
Routine diagnosis of HIV relies on antibody/antigen combination tests, not RT-PCR — PCR-based viral load testing is a monitoring/confirmatory tool used after diagnosis (or in specific situations like early infant diagnosis), not the standard first diagnostic test in most settings.
Theme 8

TORCH & transfusion-transmitted infections

Three questions (Q19, Q34, Q40) return to the transplacental/TORCH theme raised briefly in Theme 3 (CMV) — worth consolidating into one clean list, plus the related but distinct question of what can be transmitted through blood transfusion.

Mnemonic TORCH: Toxoplasma, Other (syphilis/Treponema, varicella-zoster, parvovirus B19), Rubella, CMV, HSV — the classic transplacental/congenital infection group. Organisms that primarily infect the neonate intrapartum (during birth passage) rather than transplacentally — gonorrhoea, HPV — are a deliberate contrast the exam likes to test alongside the true TORCH list.
Q40 · Oct 2012

  1. CMV
  2. VZV
  3. Treponema pallidum
  4. HPV
  5. Neisseria gonorrhoeae
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c. CMV, VZV and syphilis are classic transplacental/congenital pathogens.

d, e. HPV, gonorrhoea
Both primarily affect the neonate during passage through an infected birth canal (intrapartum), not via the placenta — the intrapartum/transplacental distinction being tested.
Q34 · Oct 2015
Transplacental infections:
  1. Neisseria gonorrhoeae
  2. Treponema pallidum
  3. HPV
  4. Varicella
  5. CMV
Show reasoning

Correct: b, d, e — the identical logic and answer set as Q40, repeated with slightly different distractors (same intrapartum-vs-transplacental contrast).

Q19
Infections transmitted with blood transfusion:
  1. Toxoplasma gondii
  2. CMV
  3. Varicella zoster virus
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b. Both Toxoplasma and CMV can hide within transfused leukocytes and transmit via blood products — this is part of the rationale behind leukoreduction and CMV-negative blood products for high-risk recipients (e.g. transplant patients, neonates).

c. VZV
Not considered a classic transfusion-transmitted infection — VZV transmission via blood is exceedingly uncommon and doesn't feature in standard transfusion-safety teaching the way CMV and Toxoplasma do.
Theme 9

Ebola virus disease

One question (Q38), but worth knowing as a distinct clinical picture from the febrile illnesses covered so far — a genuine viral haemorrhagic fever with a broader symptom complex than its "bleeding disease" reputation might suggest.

Q38
Typical features of Ebola virus infection:
  1. Frontal headache
  2. Bloody diarrhoea
  3. Maculopapular rash
  4. Severe backache
  5. Joint pain
Show reasoning

Correct: all of a–e. Ebola virus disease begins with a non-specific febrile illness — fever, severe headache, myalgia, back pain, arthralgia — that can look like many other tropical fevers early on, then progresses to a maculopapular rash and, in more severe cases, GI involvement including bloody diarrhoea and other haemorrhagic manifestations. The exam-relevant point is that early Ebola looks deceptively non-specific — the haemorrhagic features that give the disease its reputation typically appear later in the course, not at presentation.

Theme 10

How viruses actually damage host cells

One question (Q6) steps back from any single virus to ask about the general mechanisms viruses use to injure their host — a useful frame to hold underneath every specific virus discussed above.

Q6
Viruses cause reactions in hosts by:
  1. Apoptosis
  2. Reduced protein synthesis in host cells
  3. DNA mutations
  4. Metaplasia
  5. Type I hypersensitivity
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c. Direct viral cytopathic mechanisms include triggering apoptosis in infected cells, shutting down host protein synthesis (many viruses hijack ribosomal machinery for their own translation while suppressing the host's), and inducing DNA damage/mutation (directly, or via integration in the case of some retroviruses/oncoviruses).

d. Metaplasia
Not a direct, general viral cytopathic mechanism — though specific associations exist (HPV and cervical metaplasia/dysplasia, EBV and gastric changes), these are specific oncogenic pathways for particular viruses over a long time course, not a general acute cytopathic effect applicable to viruses broadly.
e. Type I hypersensitivity
An IgE-mediated allergic mechanism, unrelated to how viruses directly damage the cells they infect — a mechanism-category distractor testing whether you can distinguish direct cytopathic effects from unrelated immunological phenomena.
Theme 11

Predicted questions — test yourself

The same five virology-flavoured "predicted questions" the author built from patterns across the real past papers. They map straight back onto Themes 1, 2 and 4 above — treat them as a closed-book self-test before you move to the reference sheet.

Predicted Q1
A 68-year-old man with COVID-19 pneumonia deteriorates to severe ARDS and dies. Which histopathological features would you expect in the lungs at autopsy?
  1. Diffuse alveolar damage with hyaline membranes
  2. Widespread microthrombi in pulmonary vessels
  3. Caseating granulomas
  4. Bronchiectasis
  5. Intranuclear Cowdry A inclusions in type II pneumocytes
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b — exactly the autopsy picture built in Theme 1's mechanism callout: DAD with hyaline membranes as the final common ARDS pathway, plus microthrombi from the endothelial/ pro-thrombotic component of severe disease.

c. Caseating granulomas
The signature of TB, not a viral pneumonia — a cross-topic distractor pulling in a bacteriology pattern (Theme 5 of the Bacteriology lesson) to see if you're pattern-matching "lung pathology" rather than the specific COVID mechanism.
d. Bronchiectasis
A chronic structural airway change that develops over months-years of recurrent infection/ inflammation — not an acute autopsy finding in a patient who has just died of ARDS.
e. Cowdry A inclusions
Classic for herpesvirus infection (HSV, VZV — see Theme 3), not SARS-CoV-2. A reminder that "viral inclusion bodies" isn't a single generic finding: different virus families leave different histological fingerprints.
Predicted Q2
Regarding laboratory diagnosis of COVID-19, which of the following statements are correct?
  1. A high CT value in RT-PCR generally indicates low viral load
  2. Routine RT-PCR assays reliably identify the specific viral variant
  3. Antigen positivity correlates with higher infectivity
  4. SARS-CoV-2 total antibody is the investigation of choice in the first 3 days of illness
  5. Viral culture is routinely used to determine infectivity in clinical practice
Show reasoning

Correct: a, c — straight application of Theme 1's diagnostic-timing heuristic. CT value is inversely related to viral load (more amplification cycles needed to detect signal ⇒ less starting template ⇒ higher CT ⇒ lower load), and rapid antigen tests are most sensitive precisely when viral load — and therefore infectivity — is highest.

b. RT-PCR identifies the variant
Routine diagnostic RT-PCR detects viral RNA target genes, not the whole genome — it tells you "positive/negative" (and roughly how much virus), not which variant. Variant calling needs genomic sequencing, a separate, specialised step used for surveillance, not routine diagnosis.
d. Antibody test in first 3 days
The recurring trap from Theme 2 of the Bacteriology lesson, replayed here: antibodies take days to weeks to rise, so early illness is a job for PCR/antigen (detecting the virus itself), not serology (detecting the host response to it).
e. Routine viral culture
Viral culture is a research/reference-lab tool (and needs BSL-3 containment for SARS-CoV-2) — not part of routine clinical infectivity assessment, where CT value and antigen status serve as practical surrogates instead.
Predicted Q3
Regarding acute HIV seroconversion syndrome, which of the following are typical features?
  1. Maculopapular or vesiculopustular rash
  2. Oral hairy leukoplakia
  3. Generalized lymphadenopathy
  4. Multiple small joint arthralgia/arthritis
  5. Profound neutropenia
Show reasoning

Correct: a, c, d — the acute seroconversion picture from Theme 2's mechanism callout: a mononucleosis-like illness with fever, rash, lymphadenopathy and arthralgia, driven by the initial burst of viraemia and the immune system's first response to it.

b. Oral hairy leukoplakia
An EBV-driven lesion (Theme 3) that appears in established, advancing immunodeficiency — the opposite end of the HIV timeline from seroconversion, and the exact early-vs-late contrast Theme 2 emphasises.
e. Profound neutropenia
Not a feature of acute seroconversion; cytopenias, when they occur in HIV, are more a feature of advanced disease or drug toxicity, not the initial immune response to new infection.
Predicted Q4
Which of the following are AIDS-defining illnesses?
  1. CMV retinitis
  2. Cervical carcinoma
  3. Oral candidiasis limited to oropharynx
  4. Primary CNS lymphoma
  5. Recurrent herpes zoster limited to one dermatome
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, d — read directly off the AIDS-defining illness table in Theme 2. CMV retinitis, invasive cervical carcinoma, and primary CNS lymphoma all sit on that list.

c. Oropharyngeal candidiasis
Common in HIV and a useful marker of declining immunity, but it only becomes AIDS-defining when it extends to the oesophagus — oropharyngeal-only disease is not on the defining list, which is exactly the distinction the table draws.
e. Zoster, one dermatome
Single-dermatome zoster is common in the general population and not AIDS-defining; multidermatomal or recurrent, disseminated zoster is the pattern that raises concern for significant immunosuppression instead.
Predicted Q5
Regarding arboviral infections circulating in tropical countries, which of the following statements are correct?
  1. Secondary dengue infection can be more severe due to antibody-dependent enhancement
  2. Zika infection in pregnancy is associated with fetal microcephaly

  4. Zika virus infection is frequently asymptomatic
  5. Guillain–Barré syndrome is a recognised complication of Zika virus infection

Correct: a, b, d, e — Theme 4's dengue-vs-Zika contrast in one question: dengue's signature risk is ADE-driven severe secondary infection, while Zika's signature risks are congenital microcephaly and Guillain–Barré, occurring against a background where most Zika infection is mild or silent.

Overreach in the "commonly" — severe haemorrhagic/shock presentations are the feared but minority outcome of dengue (concentrated in secondary infection), and are not a typical feature of Zika at all, which is usually mild. Lumping the two together erases exactly the contrast the theme is testing.

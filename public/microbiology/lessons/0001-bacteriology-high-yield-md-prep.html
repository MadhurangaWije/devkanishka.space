Theme 1

Encapsulated bacteria, asplenia & meningitis CSF patterns

Two exam-favourite reasoning chains sit under this heading: "why does losing the spleen make this specific list of bugs dangerous?" and "what does the CSF profile tell me about the organism before any culture result is back?" Both are really about one idea — how the host clears a pathogen — approached from opposite ends.

Mechanism The spleen is where the immune system deals with encapsulated bacteria. Their polysaccharide capsule blocks complement deposition and phagocyte recognition, so they can only be cleared efficiently by opsonisation — antibody or complement (C3b) coating the capsule so splenic macrophages can grab it. The spleen's marginal zone is where "first exposure" antibody (mostly IgM, T-independent) is generated against these polysaccharide antigens, and where already-opsonised organisms get filtered out of the blood. Remove the spleen (or leave it non-functional, as in sickle cell disease), and encapsulated organisms can multiply to very high titres in blood before any other clearance mechanism catches up — overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI), with mortality quoted around 50% even with treatment.
Mnemonic "SHiN" for the classic encapsulated triad that defines OPSI risk: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae type b, Neisseria meningitidis. All three are also the classic causes of community-acquired bacterial (pyogenic) meningitis in adults — the same capsule biology that makes them dangerous post-splenectomy is what lets them survive briefly in CSF, which has very low native complement and antibody.
Q1 · 2022 May
Following organisms are at increased risk in a splenectomy patient:
  1. Neisseria meningitidis
  2. Listeria monocytogenes
  3. Streptococcus pneumoniae
  4. Haemophilus influenzae
  5. Klebsiella pneumoniae
Show reasoning

Correct: a, c, d. These are the encapsulated organisms the spleen would normally clear via antibody/complement-mediated opsonophagocytosis; asplenic patients lose that filter and can develop fulminant sepsis within hours.

b. Listeria monocytogenes
Not encapsulated in the same sense — it's an intracellular pathogen controlled mainly by cell-mediated immunity (T cells and macrophage activation), not splenic antibody filtering. Listeria risk goes up with defects in cellular immunity (steroids, pregnancy, elderly, transplant), not asplenia specifically. This distinction — humoral/splenic clearance vs cell-mediated clearance — is the exact trap the question is testing.
e. Klebsiella pneumoniae
Does have a capsule and can cause serious sepsis, but it is not part of the classic asplenia-risk triad tested in exams; the "SHiN" organisms are what examiners want here.
High yield Post-splenectomy prophylaxis, as commonly tested: pneumococcal, Hib and meningococcal vaccination (ideally ≥2 weeks pre-splenectomy if elective), lifelong penicillin prophylaxis in many protocols (especially children and the first 1–2 years post-splenectomy in adults), and patient education to seek urgent medical review for any fever — because OPSI can kill within hours of the first symptom.

Q5 · 2022 May
60-year-old with fever, headache and altered consciousness. CSF shows lymphocytosis, low glucose and high protein. Likely causative organism:
  1. Mycobacterium tuberculosis
  2. Neisseria meningitidis
  3. Streptococcus pneumoniae
  4. Cryptococcus neoformans
  5. Haemophilus influenzae
Show reasoning

Best single answer: d. The lymphocytic/low-glucose/high-protein pattern rules out the acute pyogenic organisms (b, c, e — these give a neutrophilic picture). Between TB and Cryptococcus, the age (60) and the phrase "likely" nudge toward Cryptococcus, which is the classic cause of subacute lymphocytic meningitis in older or immunocompromised adults — visualised directly on CSF with India ink, or confirmed faster with cryptococcal antigen (CrAg) testing, which has largely replaced India ink in modern practice because of far higher sensitivity.

Exam pattern to notice: when a question gives you the lymphocytic/low-glucose CSF pattern without more clues (no HIV status, no chronicity, no contacts), TB and Cryptococcus are the two real contenders — look for the tie-breaker detail (age, immunosuppression, duration of illness, travel/contact history) rather than assuming one is always "the" answer.

Haemophilus influenzae — beyond the vaccine-preventable headline

The Hib conjugate vaccine has made invasive type b disease rare where uptake is good, but the organism still matters clinically in two other ways worth knowing cold for an MD exam: non-typeable strains causing mucosal disease, and acquired antibiotic resistance.

Q26 · July 2020
Regarding Haemophilus influenzae:
  1. Non-typeable H. influenzae can cause invasive disease
  2. Transmitted via aerosols
  3. Ampicillin resistance is noted
  4. Chancre is caused by Haemophilus haemolyticus
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c. Unencapsulated (non-typeable) H. influenzae is actually the more common isolate today and drives otitis media, sinusitis, and exacerbations of COPD/ bronchiectasis — it doesn't have the polysaccharide capsule, so it doesn't cause the same overwhelming bacteraemic disease as type b, but it is far from harmless. Spread is respiratory droplet/aerosol. Beta-lactamase production causing ampicillin resistance is well documented and clinically important when choosing empirical therapy.

d. Chancre / H. haemolyticus
Soft chancre (chancroid) is caused by Haemophilus ducreyi, a different species entirely — a classic name-confusion distractor. H. haemolyticus is a respiratory commensal, not an STI pathogen.
Theme 2

Choosing the right diagnostic test

A recurring MD-exam question type: given an organism or a clinical syndrome, which class of test actually confirms it — direct visualisation, antigen detection, serology, or nucleic-acid amplification? The test that works depends on how much organism is present, whether it can be stained/cultured at all, and how fast the host antibody response develops.

Mechanism Direct smear and staining only works when the organism is present in reasonable numbers at an accessible site and takes up a distinguishing stain — Gram stain for pyogenic bacteria in sputum/pus, Ziehl–Neelsen (acid-fast) for mycobacteria in slit-skin smear or sputum, India ink for the capsule of Cryptococcus in CSF. It fails for organisms that are too few in number, too small to see on light microscopy, or simply don't concentrate at an easily sampled site — which is exactly when you reach for antigen tests (fast, but only positive while the antigen load is high — early infection) or serology (needs days for IgM/IgG to rise, so it's a test of the past few days-to-weeks, not "right now").
Q3 · 2022 May
Which of the following can be diagnosed with smear and staining:
  1. Cryptococcus meningitis
  2. Urine for leptospirosis
  3. Split skin smear in leprosy
  4. Sputum for bacterial pneumonia
  5. Chlamydia trachomatis urethritis
Show reasoning

Correct: a, c, d. Cryptococcus has a thick polysaccharide capsule visualised directly with India ink in CSF. Leprosy's diagnostic slit-skin smear demonstrates acid-fast bacilli with Ziehl–Neelsen staining. Gram stain of sputum remains a standard first-line test for suspected bacterial pneumonia.

b. Leptospira in urine
Leptospires are thin spirochaetes below the resolution of routine light microscopy and are shed intermittently — diagnosis instead rests on serology (MAT) or PCR, covered in depth in Theme 10.
e. Chlamydia trachomatis
An obligate intracellular organism that does not Gram stain usefully in clinical practice — diagnosis is by NAAT (nucleic acid amplification test) on urine or a genital swab.
Q10 · 2022 May
Antigen testing is used to diagnose:
  1. Active SARS-CoV-2
  2. Active malarial infection after completion of treatment
  3. Pyogenic meningitis
  4. Dengue fever, day 1
  5. Hepatitis B
Show reasoning

Correct: a, d, e. Rapid antigen tests detect viral protein directly, so they work best when antigen load is genuinely high: active SARS-CoV-2 infection, dengue NS1 antigen in the first few febrile days (before the antibody response has developed — this is precisely why NS1 is useful early, while IgM/IgG become useful later), and HBsAg for active hepatitis B infection.

b. Malaria antigen after treatment
A pitfall worth remembering clinically: malaria antigen tests (HRP-2 in particular) can stay positive for weeks after parasites are cleared, so a positive antigen test after treatment does not reliably indicate active/relapsed infection — you'd want a blood film or PCR instead.
c. Pyogenic meningitis
Diagnosed by Gram stain and culture of CSF (Theme 1), not by a generic "antigen test" — bacterial antigen agglutination tests exist historically but are not the standard confirmatory approach.
High yield Whenever an MCQ gives you "day 1 of fever," think antigen or PCR (organism/antigen is present, antibody isn't yet). Whenever it gives you "day 7–14" or "convalescent," think serology (IgM has had time to rise). This single timing heuristic resolves a large fraction of diagnostic-test MCQs across this whole deck — dengue, leptospirosis, and the viral serology questions in Theme lessons of Part 2 all follow it.
Theme 3

Toxin-mediated vs immune-mediated disease

This is the single most repeated reasoning pattern in the whole bacteriology deck (it drives Q6, Q7, Q11, Q21 and Q39 individually). The examiner keeps re-asking one question in different clothes: is the damage caused directly by a bacterial toxin, or is it caused by the host's own immune response reacting to the organism after the fact? Get this distinction solid and four separate "matching" questions collapse into one piece of reasoning.

Mechanism Toxin-mediated disease: a discrete protein exotoxin does the damage, often without needing ongoing bacterial invasion — the toxin alone (or even just ingesting preformed toxin, as in staphylococcal food poisoning) is sufficient. Onset is typically fast (hours), and the disease can sometimes be reproduced by the toxin alone in the lab.

Immune-mediated (post-infectious) disease: the organism triggers an antibody or T-cell response that then cross-reacts with host tissue (molecular mimicry) or forms immune complexes that deposit in target organs. Onset is delayed — typically 1–3 weeks after the triggering infection has already resolved, because it takes that long to mount the antibody/immune-complex response that does the damage.
Mnemonic If the disease name is a Greek/Latin word for the toxin's actual effect (scalded skin, flaccid paralysis, haemolytic uraemic), it's toxin-mediated. If the disease is named for the organ it inflames weeks later (rheumatic fever, glomerulo-nephritis, Guillain–Barré), it's immune-mediated. Toxins act fast and locally on a target; immune complications act slow and at a distance.
Q7 · 2022 May
Toxins and diseases are correctly matched:
  1. Exfoliative toxin – Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome
  2. Streptococcus pyogenes – Rheumatic fever
  3. Bacillus cereus enterotoxin – Gastroenteritis
  4. Botulinum – Spastic paralysis
  5. Shiga-like verotoxin from E. coli – Haemolytic uraemic syndrome
Show reasoning

Correct: a, c, e. Staphylococcal exfoliative toxin cleaves desmoglein-1 in the skin directly, causing scalded skin syndrome. Bacillus cereus enterotoxins act on gut epithelium to cause vomiting (emetic toxin, cereulide) or diarrhoea (diarrhoeal enterotoxin). Shiga-like verotoxin from enterohaemorrhagic E. coli damages renal endothelium directly, producing haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS).

b. Rheumatic fever
Immune-mediated, not toxin-mediated — molecular mimicry between streptococcal M protein and cardiac myosin/valve tissue, occurring 2–4 weeks after untreated pharyngitis. This is the exact contrast the question wants you to catch against option a.
d. Botulinum toxin
Blocks acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction, causing flaccid (not spastic) paralysis — compare with tetanus toxin, which blocks inhibitory neurotransmitter release and causes spastic paralysis. This toxin/paralysis-type pairing is a classic reversed distractor.
Q6 · 2022 May
Microorganisms and their non-infectious complications are correctly matched:
  1. Hepatitis A – HCC
  2. Chlamydia pneumoniae – Atherosclerosis
  3. Streptococcus pyogenes – Rheumatic fever
  4. Streptococcus pneumoniae – Nephrotic syndrome
  5. Campylobacter – Guillain–Barré syndrome
Show reasoning

Correct: c, e. Both are the classic immune-mediated ("non-infectious") complications of an infection that has often already cleared: S. pyogenes → rheumatic fever / post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (immune cross-reactivity and immune-complex deposition respectively); Campylobacter jejuni → Guillain–Barré syndrome, via molecular mimicry between C. jejuni lipo-oligosaccharide and peripheral nerve gangliosides.

a. Hepatitis A → HCC
Hepatitis A does not cause chronic infection at all (it's cleared, not carried), so it cannot drive the chronic inflammation/cirrhosis pathway to hepatocellular carcinoma the way hepatitis B and C do.
b. Chlamydia pneumoniae → atherosclerosis
A historical hypothesis (organism found in atheromatous plaques) that did not hold up in controlled trials of antibiotic therapy for cardiovascular prevention — not accepted as a proven causal link, and not the "correct" pairing examiners want.
d. S. pneumoniae → nephrotic syndrome
Not a recognised association — don't confuse with post-streptococcal (pyogenes) glomerulonephritis, which is nephritic, not nephrotic, and caused by a different Streptococcus species entirely.
Q11 · 2021 Nov
Toxin-mediated diseases:
  1. Streptococcal pharyngitis
  2. Clostridium difficile (infection)
  3. Staphylococcal food poisoning
  4. Rheumatic fever
  5. Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c. C. difficile disease is driven by toxins A and B damaging colonic epithelium (see the dedicated box below). Staphylococcal food poisoning is caused entirely by preformed enterotoxin ingested with contaminated food — onset is within 1–6 hours, far too fast to be an infective process; the bacteria themselves don't even need to still be alive.

a, d. Strep pharyngitis / rheumatic fever
Both immune-mediated, not toxin-mediated — the same trap as Q7 above, repeated.
e. SSPE
Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis is a late complication of persistent defective measles virus in the brain, years after the original infection — a slow viral phenomenon, not a bacterial toxin at all.
Q21 · Nov 2020
Bacterial toxin mediated diseases:
  1. Clostridium difficile
  2. Typhoid
  3. Scalded skin syndrome
  4. TB
  5. Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis
Show reasoning

Correct: a, c. Same underlying logic as Q7/Q11. Typhoid and TB cause disease mainly through invasion and the host granulomatous/inflammatory response to an intracellular organism, not a discrete exotoxin; post-streptococcal GN is immune-complex mediated, occurring weeks after the triggering throat or skin infection.

Q39 · Oct 2018
Toxin producing bacteria:
  1. Staphylococcus aureus
  2. Bacillus cereus
  3. Enterotoxigenic E. coli
  4. Salmonella typhimurium
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c. All three have well-characterised enterotoxins (S. aureus: heat-stable enterotoxins + TSST-1; B. cereus: emetic/diarrhoeal toxins; ETEC: heat-labile [LT] and heat-stable [ST] toxins driving watery diarrhoea via cAMP/cGMP-mediated chloride secretion).

d. Salmonella typhimurium
Causes disease predominantly through mucosal invasion and an inflammatory response, not a defined enterotoxin — the mechanistic opposite of the other three options.

Clostridioides difficile in depth

Worth its own stop, since it recurs across three separate questions in this deck (Q11, Q21, Q47) and combines toxin biology with a distinct set of microbiology/therapy facts.

Q47 · Oct 2017
Clostridium difficile:
  1. Infection diagnosed by serological testing
  2. Is a Gram-positive bacteria
  3. Infection is treated by 3rd generation cephalosporin
  4. Causes pseudomembranous colitis following metronidazole
Show reasoning

Correct: b. Clostridioides difficile is a Gram-positive, spore-forming, obligate anaerobic bacillus.

a. Serology
Diagnosis is by stool toxin immunoassay or toxin-gene PCR/NAAT (often as a two-step algorithm with GDH antigen screening) — not serology, since the disease is a local toxin effect in the gut, not a systemic antibody response worth measuring.
c. Third-generation cephalosporins
These are a major risk factor for C. difficile infection (broad-spectrum disruption of colonic flora lets C. difficile overgrow) — the exact opposite of being a treatment. Treatment is oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin (metronidazole is now reserved for milder disease/limited access settings in many guidelines).
d. Pseudomembranous colitis "following metronidazole"
Metronidazole is a treatment for C. difficile, not a cause of it — the antibiotics most strongly linked to precipitating C. difficile colitis are clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, and broad-spectrum cephalosporins/penicillins.

Bacterial spores — a dormancy fact, not a toxin fact

A slightly different idea sitting in the same neighbourhood: spores are not how bacteria cause disease, they're how spore-forming bacteria (Bacillus, Clostridium) survive hostile conditions before disease starts.

Q16 · July 2021
Bacterial spores:
  1. Is the major form existing in the body
  2. Destroyed by boiling
  3. Causes antibiotic resistance
  4. Pathogenic when ingested
Show reasoning

Correct: d. Spores of organisms like Bacillus cereus and Clostridium botulinum/perfringens can be ingested (e.g. in reheated rice, improperly canned food), survive gastric acid, and then germinate back into the vegetative, toxin-producing form in the gut.

a. Major form in the body
The opposite is true: spores are a dormant survival state formed outside the host under nutrient-poor or hostile conditions; inside an actively infected host, the organism is normally in its vegetative, metabolically active form.
b. Destroyed by boiling
Spores are strikingly resistant to boiling and most disinfectants — this is precisely why spore-related food poisoning happens (ordinary cooking/reheating fails to sterilise them) and why autoclaving (pressurised steam well above 100°C) is needed for true sterilisation.
c. Antibiotic resistance
Sporulation is a survival strategy against environmental stress (heat, desiccation, disinfectants), not a resistance mechanism against antibiotics, which is a distinct topic (efflux pumps, altered targets, enzymatic drug inactivation).
Theme 4

Antibiotic pharmacokinetics & pharmacodynamics

Five separate past questions (Q23, Q24, Q38, Q56, plus the elimination-kinetics question sitting next to Q24) all test the same underlying pharmacology skeleton: what determines whether killing a bacterium depends on the peak concentration, the total time above MIC, or the total drug exposure over 24 hours — and what that implies for how you dose the drug.

Mechanism Time-dependent killers (β-lactams: penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems) kill roughly at a fixed rate once the concentration is above the MIC — going higher above MIC doesn't kill much faster. So the goal is to keep concentration above MIC for as much of the dosing interval as possible: frequent dosing, or even continuous/extended infusions.

Concentration-dependent killers (aminoglycosides, and to some extent daptomycin, metronidazole) kill faster and more completely the higher the peak concentration is above MIC — so the goal is a high peak, which is why aminoglycosides are often given as one large daily dose rather than split doses. They also show a post-antibiotic effect (PAE): bacterial regrowth stays suppressed for a while even after the drug concentration falls below the MIC, because the aminoglycoside has already caused lasting ribosomal/protein-synthesis damage.

AUC/MIC-dependent killers (fluoroquinolones, vancomycin, azithromycin, tetracyclines) sit in between — total drug exposure over 24 hours relative to MIC predicts efficacy better than either peak or time-above-MIC alone.
PatternRepresentative drugsDosing implication
Time-dependent (%T>MIC)β-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems), clindamycin, macrolides (mostly)Frequent dosing or extended/continuous infusion; peak level doesn't matter much
Concentration-dependent (Cmax/MIC)Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin), daptomycin, metronidazoleOne large dose per interval (e.g. once-daily aminoglycoside dosing) to maximise peak
AUC/MIC-dependentFluoroquinolones, vancomycin, azithromycin, tetracyclines, tigecycline Total 24-hour exposure matters most; dosing interval is less critical than cumulative dose
Q23 · July 2020
Regarding antibiotics with time-dependent bactericidal activity:
  1. Bactericidal effect depends on the concentration above the MIC
  2. Bactericidal effect depends on the time the plasma concentration is above the MIC
  3. Frequent administration results in greater bactericidal effect
  4. Show good penetration to the site of infection
  5. Bacterial multiplication suppressed until the next dose given
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c. This is the definition of time-dependent killing directly, plus its obvious dosing consequence (more frequent dosing → more time above MIC → better effect).

a. Depends on concentration above MIC
That's the definition of concentration-dependent killing (aminoglycosides), the opposite pattern.
d. Good tissue penetration
A property of an individual drug's pharmacokinetics, not something that follows from being "time-dependent" as a pharmacodynamic class.
e. Suppressed until next dose (post-antibiotic effect)
PAE is a hallmark of concentration-dependent drugs like aminoglycosides — time-dependent β-lactams generally have a minimal PAE against Gram-negatives, which is exactly why they need frequent dosing rather than relying on lingering suppression between doses.
Q38 · Oct 2018 (True/False)
Regarding antibiotic pharmacodynamics:
  1. Penicillin shows concentration-dependent bactericidal activity
  2. Aminoglycosides show time-dependent bactericidal activity
  3. Bactericidal activity of aminoglycosides is evident at concentrations below the MIC
  4. Time-dependent drugs need frequent dosing
Show reasoning

Correct: d only. Options a and b simply swap the two patterns (penicillin is actually time-dependent; aminoglycosides are actually concentration-dependent) — a very common exam distractor format, worth spotting on sight. Option c is subtly wrong: the aminoglycoside post-antibiotic effect means regrowth stays suppressed for a while once concentration falls below MIC, but that is not the same as the drug still exerting active bactericidal killing below MIC — the killing itself still requires concentration above MIC to begin with.

Q56 · Oct 2016
Antimicrobials:
  1. ESBL acts against cephalosporins and penicillin-resistant organisms
  2. Erythromycin can be used in penicillin resistance
  3. Aminoglycoside activity is dose dependent
  4. Antibiotics with time-dependent killing should be given in frequent dosage intervals
  5. Aminoglycoside activity persists below the minimal effective concentration
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, d, e. b–e are all restatements of the concepts above (macrolides as penicillin-allergy/resistance alternatives for susceptible Gram-positives; concentration-dependent aminoglycoside killing; frequent dosing for time-dependent agents; the post-antibiotic effect meaning activity persists even once concentration has fallen).

a. ESBL wording
ESBL (extended-spectrum β-lactamase) is a bacterial resistance enzyme that hydrolyses penicillins and cephalosporins — it doesn't "act against" resistant organisms, it is the resistance mechanism. A wording trap rather than a pharmacology trap.

Kinetics of drug elimination — first-order vs zero-order

A distinct pharmacology idea that shares the same slide neighbourhood: this is about how the body eliminates a drug, not how the drug kills bacteria. Don't let the two get tangled — PK/PD killing patterns above are about antibacterial effect; elimination order below is about drug clearance and toxicity risk.

Q24 · July 2020
Regarding penicillins:
  1. MRSA is resistant to all beta-lactam antibiotics
  2. Renal clearance of penicillin is less than the glomerular filtration rate
  3. Allergic reaction to penicillin is more likely with topical than oral administration
  4. Rash developing with amoxicillin in infectious mononucleosis is itchy
  5. Piperacillin shows zero-order kinetics
Show reasoning

Correct: a only. MRSA's altered penicillin-binding protein (PBP2a, encoded by mecA) has low affinity for essentially all β-lactams, including advanced agents like meropenem — this is why anti-MRSA therapy has to leave the β-lactam class entirely (vancomycin, linezolid, etc. — Theme 6).

b. Renal clearance vs GFR
Wrong direction — penicillins are cleared by both glomerular filtration and active tubular secretion, so total renal clearance exceeds GFR, not falls below it (this is also why probenecid, which blocks tubular secretion, prolongs penicillin levels).
c. Topical vs oral allergy risk
The reverse is generally accepted — topical application is more sensitising for many drugs, but this option overstates/misstates the accepted teaching point about penicillin specifically, and isn't the tested correct answer here.
d. Amoxicillin rash in mononucleosis
Classically described as a non-itchy, morbilliform rash — a distinct, non-IgE-mediated phenomenon related to the underlying EBV infection, not a true penicillin allergy, which is exactly why it doesn't necessarily preclude future penicillin use.
e. Piperacillin kinetics
Piperacillin follows ordinary first-order elimination like most antibiotics at therapeutic doses — zero-order kinetics is the exception (saturated pathways), not the rule, and isn't a recognised feature of piperacillin.
Theme 5

Anti-tuberculous chemotherapy

TB drugs are tested repeatedly (Q2, Q44) plus a standalone reference table on the newer MDR/XDR-TB drug groups. The organising idea worth holding in your head: Mycobacterium tuberculosis exists in your body in several metabolic states at once — actively dividing, semi-dormant, and dormant within caseous material — and different first-line drugs are chosen precisely because each hits a different one of those populations.

Mechanism Isoniazid (H) is most active against rapidly dividing extracellular bacilli — it's the most potent early bactericidal drug, and its high lipid solubility gives it excellent CSF penetration, making it central to TB meningitis regimens. Rifampicin (R) is unique in killing semi-dormant ("persister") organisms with intermittent metabolic activity — this is why it's the backbone of short-course regimens and why omitting it (as in many drug-resistant strains) lengthens treatment so dramatically. Pyrazinamide (Z) works best in the acidic, low-pH environment inside macrophages and caseous foci — it's the "sterilising" drug for intracellular dormant organisms, active mainly in the first 2 months. Ethambutol (E) is added mainly to prevent resistance emerging to the other three during the intensive phase.
Q2 · 2022 May
Anti-TB drugs:
  1. Isoniazid penetrates well into the CSF
  2. Rifampicin is effective against the semi-dormant form of mycobacterium
  3. Rifampicin is known to cause hyperuricaemia

  5. Pyrazinamide is not hepatotoxic

Theme 6

Staph aureus, MRSA & antibiotic-organism matching traps

A cluster of five questions (Q4, Q12, Q28, Q32, Q46) that together test the same underlying resistance mechanism from every angle: what PBP2a actually does, who carries MRSA and where, and which drugs remain reliable once you know beta-lactams are off the table.

Mechanism MRSA doesn't destroy the antibiotic (that's what beta-lactamases like ESBL do) — it acquires an altered penicillin-binding protein, PBP2a (encoded by the mecA/mecC gene on the SCCmec cassette), which has such low affinity for beta-lactams that the drug simply can't bind and stop cell-wall synthesis, no matter how potent or "advanced" the beta-lactam is. This is why meropenem, a very broad-spectrum carbapenem, still fails against MRSA — the resistance isn't about spectrum, it's about the drug's entire binding target being changed.

Theme 8

Blood cultures: indications & technique

Three questions (Q8, Q20, Q59) test a genuinely clinical skill: recognising which syndromes actually need blood cultures to make the diagnosis, and understanding the difference between continuous/persistent bacteraemia (where a single positive culture may be diagnostic) and intermittent/transient bacteraemia (where you need multiple, well-timed samples, and a single positive result is much easier to dismiss as contamination).

Mechanism Persistent bacteraemia happens when there's an intravascular source constantly seeding the bloodstream — infective endocarditis (organisms embedded in a vegetation on a heart valve) and infected vascular grafts are the classic examples. Because the seeding never stops, cultures are reliably positive whenever drawn, and a single positive bottle carries real diagnostic weight — except that for viridans streptococci specifically, guidelines still require multiple positive sets (part of the modified Duke criteria) before diagnosing endocarditis, because these organisms are also common skin/culture contaminants.

Intermittent bacteraemia happens with a focal, non-vascular source (pyelonephritis, abscess) that sheds organisms into the blood in bursts rather than continuously — so a single negative culture doesn't rule anything out, and timing/repeated sampling matters more.

Theme 10

Leptospirosis

Leptospirosis appears five times (Q9, Q17, Q33, Q34, Q51) — unsurprising for a Sri Lankan microbiology exam, given its endemic importance there. The exam consistently tests three things: the two-phase natural history (leptospiraemic vs immune phase), the timing of each diagnostic test relative to those phases, and the organism's zoonotic transmission biology.

Mechanism Leptospira are maintained in the renal tubules of reservoir animals (rodents, cattle, dogs) and shed in urine; humans are accidental, dead-end hosts infected via mucosal or broken-skin contact with contaminated water/soil (not sustained human-to-human transmission).

Phase 1 — leptospiraemic (septicaemic) phase (roughly the first week): the organism is directly present in blood — this is when culture and PCR are useful, while antibody tests are still typically negative because the immune response hasn't developed yet.

Phase 2 — immune phase (roughly week 2 onward): the organism has largely cleared from blood, and disease is now driven by the developing antibody response and immune-mediated vascular injury — this is when serology (MAT) becomes positive and when the severe immune-phase complications (aseptic meningitis, pulmonary haemorrhage, severe renal failure) tend to appear.

Theme 12

Leprosy

Mycobacterium leprae disease (Q36, Q42) and its treatment-related immune reactions (Q48 table) form a tight cluster. The single organising idea: leprosy's clinical spectrum is a direct readout of the host's cell-mediated immune response to the organism, from strong (tuberculoid, few organisms, well-formed granulomas) to weak (lepromatous, high bacillary load, poorly formed granulomas) — and the "reactions" are what happens when that immune balance shifts suddenly, often triggered by treatment itself.

Q36 · Oct 2018
Regarding leprosy:
  1. Mean incubation period of lepromatous leprosy is 7 years
  2. Lepra reactions can occur before treatment
  3. There is a bimodal age distribution with high incidence among 14–20 years and 40–45 years
  4. Perianal lesions characteristically seen in borderline leprosy
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c. Leprosy has one of the longest incubation periods in human infectious disease (years, sometimes decades) — lepromatous forms averaging around 7 years is a commonly cited figure. Lepra reactions are not exclusively treatment-triggered; they can occur before treatment starts, during it, or after stopping. A bimodal age distribution (young adults and middle-age) is a recognised epidemiological pattern.

d. Perianal lesions in borderline leprosy
Not a characteristic distribution — leprosy skin lesions classically favour cooler body sites (extremities, face, ears), consistent with M. leprae's preference for lower temperatures; perianal skin is not a classic site.
Q42 · April 2018
Regarding Mycobacterium leprae:
  1. Causes CNS infection
  2. Known to cause lepra reactions during treatment
  3. Tuberculoid leprosy is seen in people with low immunity
Show reasoning

Correct: b only.

a. CNS infection
M. leprae targets peripheral nerves (it prefers cooler temperatures, found in superficial nerves and skin) — it does not cause central nervous system infection.
c. Tuberculoid leprosy and immunity
Inverted — tuberculoid leprosy reflects strong cell-mediated immunity (few organisms, vigorous granulomatous response); it's lepromatous leprosy that reflects weak/absent cell-mediated immunity against M. leprae specifically (with high bacillary load and poorly organised granulomas).

Theme 13

Melioidosis

Burkholderia pseudomallei (Q22, Q43) earns its own theme because it's a favourite "great mimicker" organism in tropical medicine exams — it can look like TB, present as multiple abscesses in almost any organ, and needs a treatment duration most clinicians underestimate on first guess.

Mechanism B. pseudomallei is a Gram-negative, environmental saprophyte living in soil and surface water in endemic regions (Southeast Asia, Northern Australia, increasingly reported in South Asia). It's a facultative intracellular organism capable of surviving within phagocytes, which contributes to its tendency to cause chronic, relapsing, multi-organ abscess disease that can closely mimic TB — hence the nickname "the great mimicker."
Q22 · Nov 2020
Regarding melioidosis:
  1. Presents as clinically tuberculosis-like history
  2. Should be treated for more than 12 weeks
  3. Causes multiple lung abscesses
  4. Responds to treatment with gentamicin
  5. Caused by Gram-negative bacilli
Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, c, e. Melioidosis can present chronically with fever, weight loss and pulmonary abscesses/cavitation strongly resembling TB. Treatment is genuinely prolonged — an intensive IV phase (weeks) followed by an eradication oral phase, together often exceeding 12 weeks total, reflecting the organism's intracellular persistence and relapse risk if under-treated.

d. Gentamicin
B. pseudomallei is intrinsically resistant to aminoglycosides (among several other antibiotic classes) — treatment instead relies on ceftazidime or carbapenems (intensive phase) followed by cotrimoxazole (eradication phase).
Q43 · April 2018
Regarding melioidosis:
  1. Caused by Gram-negative bacteria
  2. Causative organisms are saprophytes
  3. Metronidazole is used in treatment
  4. Causes multiple liver abscesses

Correct: a, b, d. Multi-organ abscess formation (liver, spleen, prostate, lung) is a hallmark of disseminated disease.

No role — melioidosis is not an anaerobic infection; treatment is ceftazidime/carbapenems then cotrimoxazole, as above.
Theme 14

Enteric fever & non-typhoidal Salmonella

Four questions (Q29, Q30, Q52, Q54) draw a sharp, exam-favourite line between Salmonella Typhi/Paratyphi causing systemic enteric fever, and non-typhoidal Salmonella serovars causing localised gastroenteritis (usually) with occasional invasive disease as a red flag.

Mechanism Salmonella Typhi/Paratyphi have adapted to invade through gut lymphoid tissue (Peyer's patches), survive inside macrophages, and disseminate systemically via the reticuloendothelial system — producing a primary bacteraemia during incubation (largely silent) and a secondary bacteraemia that coincides with the onset of clinical fever, which is why blood culture is most useful early in illness. Non-typhoidal Salmonella serovars, by contrast, are typically zoonotic, usually stay localised to the gut causing self-limiting gastroenteritis — invasive/recurrent disease from a non-typhoidal serovar is unusual enough that it should prompt you to look for an underlying immune defect (HIV, sickle cell disease/functional asplenia, extremes of age).
Q29 · Oct 2019
Regarding non-typhoid Salmonella:

  3. Recurrent systemic illness is an indication to exclude immune deficiency
  4. Oral antibiotics are needed for gastroenteritis
  5. Is a zoonosis
Show reasoning

Correct: c, e. As above — recurrent/systemic non-typhoidal Salmonella should trigger an immune-deficiency work-up, and these serovars are classically zoonotic (poultry, eggs, reptiles).

a. S. Paratyphi A
Causes enteric (paratyphoid) fever, the typhoidal syndrome — it is explicitly not classified as non-typhoidal, despite the name-similarity trap.
b. Meningitis
Not a typical feature of non-typhoidal Salmonella infection in the way it is for the classic meningitis organisms in Theme 1.
d. Oral antibiotics for gastroenteritis
Routine uncomplicated non-typhoidal Salmonella gastroenteritis is usually self-limiting and does not require antibiotics — treatment can even prolong carriage in some cases; antibiotics are reserved for severe, invasive, or high-risk-host disease.

Theme 15

Infective diarrhoea: watery vs bloody

Three questions (Q49, Q55, Q57) plus a reference table on E. coli pathotypes turn on one clinical distinction: watery (secretory/toxin-driven) vs bloody/mucoid (invasive/ inflammatory) diarrhoea. Once you sort organisms into those two buckets, "which organism causes bloody diarrhoea" questions answer themselves.

Mechanism Watery diarrhoea: the organism (or its toxin) triggers net fluid/electrolyte secretion from intact small-bowel epithelium without much tissue destruction — Vibrio cholerae, ETEC, rotavirus, norovirus. Bloody/inflammatory diarrhoea (dysentery): the organism actually invades colonic mucosa (Shigella, EIEC, Salmonella when invasive, Entamoeba histolytica) or damages it via a cytotoxin (Shiga toxin from STEC, C. difficile toxins) — the resulting mucosal destruction and inflammation is what produces blood, mucus, and the tenesmus/urgency typical of colitis, as opposed to the high-volume watery output of small-bowel secretory disease.
Q49 · April 2017

  3. Bacillus cereus
  4. Clostridium difficile
  5. Norovirus

Correct: b, d. Campylobacter jejuni is inflammatory/invasive and commonly bloody; C. difficile toxins damage colonic mucosa causing pseudomembranous colitis, which can be bloody in severe cases.

a, c, e. Staph, B. cereus, norovirus
All classic toxin-mediated, watery causes (preformed toxin in Staph/B. cereus food poisoning; viral secretory mechanism in norovirus) — no significant mucosal invasion, so no blood.

Theme 17

Antifungals — quick hits

One question (Q45) but worth a clean pass, since antifungal facts are easy to confuse with each other and rarely get their own dedicated study time relative to antibacterials.

  1. Clotrimazole – vaginal candidiasis
  2. Ketoconazole – induces liver enzymes
  3. Fluconazole – poorly absorbed by gut
  4. Nystatin – systemic infections

Show reasoning

Correct: a, b, e. Clotrimazole is a standard topical/vaginal azole for candidiasis. Theme 18

Commensal flora turning pathogenic

One question (Q14), but a genuinely important clinical-reasoning category: recognising disease that arises not from an exotic new pathogen, but from the body's own normal flora crossing into a site or context it doesn't belong in.

Q14 · 2021 Nov
Infections originating from normal commensal flora:
  1. Bacterial meningitis
  2. Cervicofascial actinomycosis
  3. Subacute bacterial endocarditis
  4. Oral candidiasis
  5. Aspiration pneumonia
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, d, e. Actinomyces is normal oral flora that causes disease only once mucosal integrity is breached (dental trauma/extraction) — cervicofascial actinomycosis is the textbook example. Viridans streptococci (oral flora) seeding a damaged/prosthetic heart valve during transient bacteraemia (even from routine activities like chewing or brushing) is exactly how subacute bacterial endocarditis classically arises. Oral candidiasis is overgrowth of normally present Candida once local or systemic host defences are weakened (antibiotics, inhaled steroids, immunosuppression). a. Bacterial meningitis
The classic pyogenic meningitis organisms (Theme 1: pneumococcus, meningococcus, Hib) are typically acquired exogenously via respiratory transmission and cause invasive disease as primary pathogens — they're not being described here as opportunistic overgrowth of the patient's own existing flora in the way the other four options are.

Kikuchi disease — the infectious mimic

A fitting closer for the bacteriology deck (Q60): a condition that isn't infective at all, but presents so convincingly like one (fever, tender cervical lymphadenopathy) that it earns a place in every febrile-lymphadenopathy differential — and is worth knowing precisely so you don't chase an infectious cause indefinitely in a self-limiting condition.

Q60 · Oct 2015
Kikuchi Disease:
  1. More common in males
  2. Fever is a major manifestation
  3. Cervical lymphadenopathy is characteristic
  4. Treated with Azathioprine
  5. Settles spontaneously
Show reasoning

Correct: b, c, e. Kikuchi–Fujimoto disease (histiocytic necrotising lymphadenitis) presents with fever and tender cervical lymphadenopathy, and is characteristically self-limiting — resolving spontaneously over weeks to months without specific treatment, which is itself a key diagnostic and reassurance point.

a. More common in males
It's actually more common in females, typically young adults — a straightforward epidemiology-reversal distractor.
d. Azathioprine
Not required — the self-limiting course means supportive care (analgesia/antipyretics) is generally sufficient; immunosuppressive therapy is reserved for rare severe or recurrent cases, not routine management.
High yield Keep Kikuchi disease on your differential for "fever + tender cervical nodes" alongside infectious causes (EBV, toxoplasmosis, TB lymphadenitis, bacterial lymphadenitis) and autoimmune causes (SLE, which Kikuchi disease can occasionally herald or coexist with) — the exam is really testing whether you can hold a non-infectious mimic in mind even inside what looks like an infectious-disease question bank.
